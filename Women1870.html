Homesteading

Beginning around 1870 (and continuing on into 1914) many Canadian women got married and then moved across Canada to take advantage of the Dominion Lands Policy. Land was almost free and many families jumped at the opportunity to build their lives on this land. They headed to the Prairies in droves and arrived to find that succeeding was surviving.

These families had to start with nothing - not even shelter or readily available water. Women worked alongside their husbands and children to build a home, start a farm, and dig wells. Women were a key part of the homestead life and as such they got their hands dirty. They spun their own yarn and sewed clothing for the family, tended to the livestock and handled gardening. They did this all far away from their family and friends. They were often far from medical help as well, so injuries often resulted in death.

Owning Property and Patriarchy

In 1872, women in Ontario were given the right to earn their own wage without it being in the control of their husband. This was given to them by the Married Women's Property Act of Ontario. It was one of the first times that women were given such a right.

First Nations women were not seeing such positive change. The Indian Act of 1876 stripped native women of their Indian status and right to live on reserves if they decided to marry a non-native. This meant that if their husband died or abandoned them, they could not return to their reserve as they were essentially evicted. They were denied education and health care and they couldn't even inherit land from family that was still on the reserve. This didn't apply to native men, who could marry whoever they wanted without it resulting in loss of property or their title.

Women's Associations

Women began to form groups in order to discuss issues pertaining to their rights. One of these early organizations, Toronto Women's Literary Guild (later renamed Canadian Women's Suffrage Association) was founded by Emily Stowe, among others. Stowe was the first woman to practice medicine in Canada. She did so without having an official license, because women were not welcome in Canadian colleges and universities. She had to study in the United States to get a degree. The Toronto Women's Literary Guild worked hard as the first suffragette group in Canada to push for higher education for Women.
