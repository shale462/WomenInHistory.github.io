Family Planning

Birth control was nowhere near realization in 1890. In fact, a federal law was put in place that made it illegal to sell or advertise birth control to anyone in Canada. Birth control was viewed as immoral and could result in a woman being sent to jail for up to two years if they attempted to educate each other. (cpha.ca) This was devastating to many women who were unable to avoid having more children, and would end up with yearly pregnancies that put their health at risk and made families poor.


National Council of Women of Canada

In 1983 the National Council of Women of Canada was founded. Among the founders were Dr. Augusta Stowe-Gullen, the first woman to obtain a medical degree, as well as Henrietta Muir Edwards, one of the Famous Five. The National Council of Women of Canada was a contributor to the Act to Confer the Electoral Franchise Upon Women (which didn’t occur until 1918). The council was formed in the Horticultural Pavilion in Allan Gardes, Toronto with 1500 women in attendance. The desire to be able to vote was beginning to gain significant steam.

During this time many bills were being introduced to get women the vote in Manitoba, Nova Scotia, Ontario and Quebec. They were all defeated.


Women's Associations

Women began to form groups in order to discuss issues pertaining to their rights. One of these early organizations, Toronto Women's Literary Guild (later renamed Canadian Women's Suffrage Association) was founded by Emily Stowe, among others. Stowe was the first woman to practice medicine in Canada. She did so without having an official license, because women were not welcome in Canadian colleges and universities. She had to study in the United States to get a degree. The Toronto Women's Literary Guild worked hard as the first suffragette group in Canada to push for higher education for Women.


Victorian Order of Nurses

In 1897 the Victorian Order of Nurses (VON) was found with the help of Henrietta Edwards. The VON believed there was a desperate need for women to take up a career in nursing in order to bring health care to those who lived in remote areas. They had heard many stories of husbands whose wives had died in childbirth because they couldn’t reach help in time. The VON established what was called “cottage hospitals” in these remote areas in order to bring health care to those in need.
